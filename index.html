Overview

Fines Over Time

The Rising Tide of Mobile Fines

Imagine getting fined for using your phone while driving in 2008. Your chances were slim - police had to physically see you. Today? Sophisticated cameras watch nearly every major road. This dramatic shift has rewritten enforcement across Australia. Over fifteen years, what began as occasional police stops has become a high-tech enforcement machine. Mobile phone fines in Australia exploded by 1,700% - from 156,000 (2008) to 2.8 million (2023). What caused this seismic shift?

Australia's mobile phone fines evolved through three phases:

  • 1. The Human Eye Era (2008-2015): Steady enforcement limited by police visibility
  • 2. The Transition Dip (2016-2019) Declining manual enforcement before tech took over
  • 3. The Camera Age (2020-2023): Exponential growth where technology was adopted

You can use this chart to track your state's enforcement journey over time, compare how different detection methods changed the game and see the exact year cameras transformed enforcement in your region.

    The Age of Fines

    Who's Getting Caught - And How

    Australia's 2023 fine data reveals three striking trends. The bulk of penalties (41%, totaling over 23,000 fines) targeted drivers aged 26-39 - working professionals balancing jobs and family responsibilities. Enforcement varied dramatically by location, with Victoria accounting for 55% of national fines (31,342) while the Northern Territory issued just 205. Most surprisingly, 37 fines went to under-16s who shouldn't legally be driving, compared to only 134 penalties for Tasmania's drivers aged 65+.

        Fine Hotspots

        Where and Who Gets Fined - The Urban Youth Phenomenon

        Australia's mobile phone fines reveal that young drivers in cities get more mobile phone fines than those in rural areas, showing a clear difference between urban and rural regions

        • Urban areas dominate enforcement with major cities issuing 90% of fines - particularly targeting young professionals (70% of fines hit drivers under 40)
        • Remote regions show minimal penalties with outback areas recording less than 1% of national fines, raising questions about detection gaps
        • Most surprisingly, even underage non-drivers appear in the data with one under-16 fined in cities while Tasmania's entire 65+ population received just 134 fines

        This geographic breakdown shows enforcement isn't just about safety - it's about which roads get monitored.
